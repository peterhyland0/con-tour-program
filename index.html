President, Cork Constitution F.C.

Dear Members, Players, and Supporters,

It gives me great pleasure to welcome you to our 2026 tour program. This year marks another significant milestone in our club's proud history, and I am delighted that we have the opportunity to represent Cork Constitution F.C. on the international stage once again.

Our tour to Boston represents more than just a series of matches; it embodies the spirit of camaraderie, excellence, and tradition that has defined our club for generations. As we embark on this journey, we carry with us the hopes and best wishes of our entire club community.

I would like to extend my sincere gratitude to our coaching staff, management team, and all the volunteers who have worked tirelessly to make this tour possible. Their dedication and commitment to our players' development, both on and off the field, is truly commendable.

I wish everyone associated with this tour the very best. May it be a successful and memorable experience for all involved.

With warm regards,

Ralph Keyes

Feature Story

Reflections Ahead Of The Tour

Donal Linehan — Sports Correspondent

Donal Linehan offers a considered take on the club's preparation, history and the meaning of a tour away from home.

The Cork Con tour has always been more than a sequence of fixtures; it is a living, breathing expression of a community. In the weeks leading up to departure you can feel the club's heartbeat — training grounds buzzing, kitrooms alive with purpose, and an optimism that is part ritual, part necessity.

On the face of it, a tour is about rugby: patterns, fitness, and the occasional great victory. But the less visible work — the conversations on the bus, the shared meals, the quiet encouragement after a testing session — are what shape the squad into more than the sum of its players.

The management team have put significant thought into balancing playing time with rest, and the schedule reflects an awareness that player welfare underpins long-term success. That attention to detail can be the difference between a team that arrives fatigued and one that returns with memories and lessons intact.

"Touring exposes character. The strongest squads are those who treat every day away as part of one shared performance."

There is also the wider backdrop: from supporters who fundraise to local partners who open doors abroad. Touring remains an expensive endeavour, yet it yields returns that are social as much as sporting. Young players gain confidence. Senior players find renewed leadership responsibilities. The club's profile is quietly elevated.

At the centre of it all are the players themselves: a group whose commitment to wearing the jersey transcends the scoreboard. For many, these tours are formative chapters — moments that inform careers, friendships and a lifelong affinity with the club.

Beyond the pitch, the support network that surrounds the squad is quietly heroic: volunteers who organise travel logistics, families who host visiting players, and alumni who offer counsel. These contributions create an environment in which young players can focus on growth without being consumed by the practicalities of touring.

Preparation also involves small rituals that bind a team together — a shared warm-up routine, a pre-match playlist, or a simple handshake that signals concentration. It is in these repeated gestures that group culture is formed and leadership is tested.

Looking ahead, the club's aim is not only to compete but to return enriched: players who have learned, supporters who have stories to tell, and relationships abroad that open future opportunities for exchange and development.

Feature Story

Reflections Ahead Of The Tour

Donal Linehan — Sports Correspondent

Continued from previous page

There is a delicate craft to blending ambition with humility. Teams that tour well are those that respect the opposition, the host culture and the simple logistics of travel. Plan well, listen to your bodies, and treat every encounter as an opportunity to learn.

Each tour becomes a catalogue of small victories, not all of them recorded on a scoreboard: a player overcoming a setback, a collective recovery after a tough fixture, and the quiet satisfactions of routine done well. These moments, stitched together, define the narrative that the club will recount in years to come.

The moments of levity are no less important: a late-night sing-song, a local café discovery, the relief of a shared joke after a long day. These memories stitch themselves into the fabric of a team's identity, long after the scoreboard has been forgotten.

As the squad steps onto foreign soil, there will be nerves and excitement in equal measure. What matters most is that the club leaves behind not only a record of matches played, but an impression of character — sportsmanship, courtesy, and the warmth that visitors carry back as ambassadors of Cork Con.

When the tour concludes, conversations will shift from analysis to reflection. The best measure of success will be the stories told years from now, and the knowledge that a group of players and supporters did something together that mattered.

In quiet moments between fixtures, players often take stock of small improvements: sharper passing lines, better set-piece communication, or marginal gains in conditioning. These incremental steps rarely make the headlines, but they are the true currency of progress.

Coaches and support staff emphasise continuity — creating routines that survive travel disruptions and short recovery windows. This kind of disciplined consistency helps a touring side remain steady when plans inevitably change.

Beyond the team bubble, the local communities that welcome visiting sides add texture to the experience. Friendly encounters at cafés or with youth teams can be as meaningful as any result on the field — they remind players why the sport matters at a human level.

For younger squad members, the chance to see different coaching approaches and local playing styles is invaluable. Exposure broadens tactical understanding and nurtures adaptability — traits that serve a player well long after the tour ends.

Ultimately, tours succeed when planning, character and goodwill converge. This year's trip will be measured in memories, lessons learned and the quiet strengthening of relationships that will support Cork Con for seasons to come.

A message from Jonny Holland

Head Coach, Cork Constitution F.C.

Dear Squad,

As we prepare to embark on this exciting tour to Boston, I want to take a moment to share my thoughts and expectations for the journey ahead. This tour represents an incredible opportunity for each of you to showcase your talent, develop as players, and strengthen the bonds that make our squad special.

Throughout this tour, I expect every player to demonstrate the core values that define Cork Constitution rugby: discipline, commitment, and respect. On the field, we will compete with intensity and intelligence. Off the field, you are ambassadors for our club and your conduct should reflect the proud tradition we represent.

Training sessions will be demanding, and match days will test your character. Embrace these challenges. Push yourselves beyond what you think is possible. Support your teammates, learn from every experience, and remember that growth happens when we step outside our comfort zones.

To the leadership group, I'm counting on you to set the standard. Your actions speak louder than words, and your teammates will follow your example. Lead with purpose, communicate clearly, and never forget the responsibility that comes with wearing the captain's armband or being a senior player.

This tour is about more than rugby. It's about creating memories, building character, and representing our club with pride. Give everything you have in every session, every match, and every moment. That's all I ask.

Let's make this tour one to remember.

Up Cork Con,

Jonny Holland
